# Meet the Family

Tool use, extremely social, exceptional vision (including many species that can discern between three colours rather than just two), there are many factors that make primates stand out from their other mammalian relatives, and that's before you even get to us. But the extended family is full of weirdos too. Look at the phylogeny on the left, it shows the evolutionary relationships between Euarchonta as we currently understand them. What the hell is a Euarchonta? Fair question, it is the group that contains primates (apes, monkeys, tarsiers, lemurs, lorises and galagos), and their closest relatives the colugos (flying lemurs) and treeshrews. Native to southern Asiatic jungles, both the colugos and treeshrews are small groups, not very diverse and with only a limited fossil record as well. They are arboreal (tree-living), as are many of their primate cousins, and (by our best estimates) these groups split from one another roughly 75-70 Million years ago (Ma), only just before a rock from outerspace smashed into Earth and changed the game completely.
